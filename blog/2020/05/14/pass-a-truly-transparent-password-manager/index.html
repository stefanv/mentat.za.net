pass: A truly transparent password manager

May 14, 2020
#

Hopefully, with phishing being as prevalent as it is, no one still uses their birthday (or any other single password) to access all their online services. Indeed, services get compromised all the time, which means that your email address and perhaps even your password already got leaked. Here, check for yourself.

A few years ago, I started signing up for services with unique email addresses of the form servicename@domain-i-own.com, to track where spammers obtained my address. You’d be surprised at how many non-solicited emails I receive on those addresses, even from ones tied to reputable companies.

What you can and definitely should do is to use a unique password for each site. There are numerous tools that help you to generate and remember such passwords, such as LastPass, 1Password, and Firefox Lockwise. However, since I prefer to (a) keep my systems simple and transparent and (b) prefer not to rely on external services, I have found two other password management systems that work well:

  1. KeePassX

    KeePassX is a cross-platform open source application that stores your passwords in an encrypted file on your hard-drive. It has a great graphical user interface that makes it easy to pull up passwords and fill them where necessary. It also allows you to store secret files as attachments.

What do I recommend / use?

I typically recommend different password managers depending on the technical level of sophistication of the person asking. LastPass for the general public, KeePassX for tech-savvy users, and pass for users who enjoy their terminal and may already have GPG keys.
